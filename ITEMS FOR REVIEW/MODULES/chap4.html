Statistical Hypothesis Testing for Large Samples

Statistical hypothesis testing is quite possibly one of the most important scientific advances of the last 100 years. It created a means of assessing evidence in the presence of uncertainty or randomness and provided a means of testing the “reasonableness” of scientific statements and theories.

Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing refers to the practice of testing scientific hypotheses using data and taking into account the uncertainty in the data. Hypothesis testing relies on both a set of scientifically valid hypotheses and a statistical model for evaluating the data and assessing the hypotheses.

Falsifiability

Falsifiablity
Consider the assertion that ghosts or spirits are real. Is this a falsifiable hypothesis?

The modern basis for scientific reasoning can be attributed to the work of philosopher Karl Popper. Popper created the criteria for defining a hypothesis as “scientific” if it could be shown to be falsifiable, or able to be proven incorrect.

Example:
Consider the assertion that ghosts or spirits are real. Is this a falsifiable hypothesis?

Any attempt to disprove this hypothesis can be countered with the argument that the lack of evidence of existence is not evidence of non-existence, i.e. Just becuase you don’t observe a ghost dowsn’t mean that they don’t exist, it just means that you didn’t see one. So the statement is not falsifiable, hence this is not a valid hypothesis.

Conversely, people used to believe that all swans were white. To disprove this hypothesis would only require one example of a non-white swan. In 1697 the Dutch explorer Willem de Vlamingh sailed up what is now known as the Swan River in Western Australia and saw black swans. The term “black swan” is now used to refer to unexpected, rare (and usually extreme) events.

The Neyman-Pearson Lemma and Hypothesis Testing

The Neyman-Pearson Lemma is a formal mathematical proof that guides how to construct a hypothesis test. The details of the lemma are beyond the scope of this unit.

The central idea of the Neyman-Pearson lemma is:

A test is constructed based on the hypotheses \[ H_0: \mbox{The null hypothesis}\qquad H_A:\mbox{The alternative hypothesis}. \] A test statistic that is a function of the observed data. \[ T(\mathbf{x}). \]

And a rejection region or a set of possible values of the test statistic such that if the test statistic falls in that region, the null hypothesis is rejected.

The hypotheses are designed such that the null hypothesis is a falsifiable statement. The null hypothesis is typically a statement that there is no effect or that a parameter is equal to some value. The alternative hypothesis is the complement to the null hypothesis, or that the null hypothesis is not true.

The test statistic is a function of observations modelled as random variables and as such, has its own sampling distribution. We can use this sampling distribution to identify the rejection region or make decisions to reject or fail to reject null hypotheses depending on how “probable” the test statistic assuming that the null hypothesis is true.

A rejection region is defined, such that if the null hypothesis is true, the probability that the test statistic falls in the rejection region is sufficiently small as to suggest that the preponderance of the evidence indicates that that the null hypothesis is false.

Hypothesis Testing and Errors

Hypothesis tests consist of a set of hypotheses, a test statistic \(T(\mathbf{x})\) and the resulting rejection region. A hypothesis test is either two-tailed (also known as a point null hypotheses): \[ H_0:\theta=\theta_0\qquad H_A:\theta\neq \theta_0 \] or single-tailed \[ H_0:\theta\leq \theta_0\qquad H_A:\theta>\theta_0 \] or \[ H_0:\theta\geq \theta_0\qquad H_A:\theta<\theta_0. \] The rejection region is chosen so that \(Pr(T(\mathbf{x}\in\mbox{Rejection Region})=\alpha\) where \(\alpha\) is the Type I error rate \[ \alpha = Pr(\mbox{Reject }H_0|H_0\mbox{ is True}). \]. The Type II error rate, \(\beta\), is \[ Pr(\mbox{Fail to Reject }H_0 |H_0\text{ is False}) \]. The compliment to the Type II error rate \(1-\beta\) is \[ 1-\beta = Pr(\mbox{Reject }H_0|H_0\mbox{ is False}) \] and is called the power of a test. We will see that power is an important design consideration when planning a study. We can understand \(\alpha\), \(\beta\) and power as the probabilities of various outcomes:

\(H_0\) is True \(H_0\) is False
Reject \(H_0\) \(\alpha\) (Type I Error Rate) 1-\(\beta\) (Power)
Fail to Reject \(H_0\) 1-\(\alpha\) \(\beta\) (Type II Error Rate)

The interpretation of all this is that in hypothesis testing we reject the null hypothesis, if the test statistic, which is a function of the data is too improbable, given the assumption that the null hypothesis is true. Note that as in the case of confidence intervals, hypothesis testing is based on probability statements about the test statistic or data, not the parameter.

Hypothesis Testing for the Population Mean

Example: Ore Refining Operations
An ore processing facility has a record of producing an average of \(880\) tons of refined ore per day. Process engineers would like to see if this has changed over time. The records from \(n=50\) days randomly selected over the last year indicate that the sample average is \(\bar{x}=871\) tons with a standard deviation of \(21\) tons. Is there evidence to assume that the output has changed? Assume a Type I error rate of \(\alpha = 0.05\).

Example: Equal Pay
Gender imbalance in pay is a concern for ensuring equitable treatment of individuals. Data for employees at a large company show that for employees in a certain role, the average weekly salary for men is $725. A sample of \(n=40\) female employees show that they earn an average weekly salary of $670 with a standard deviation of $102. Does this data support the hypothesis that women are paid at least as well as men? Assume a Type I error rate of \(\alpha = 0.10\).

The hypothesis test for the population mean is based on the sample statistic \(\bar{x}\) with the sampling distribution \[\bar{x}\sim N\left(\mu,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).\] For simplicity we define the rejection region in terms of the test statistic \[\begin{aligned} Z&=&\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\\ Z&\sim& N(0,1)\end{aligned}\] where we can define the rejection region(s) depending on the null hypothesis

Null Hypothesis Rejection Region
\(H_0: \mu=\mu_0\) \(|Z|>Z_{\alpha/2}\)
\(H_0: \mu \leq\mu_0\) \(Z>Z_\alpha\)
\(H_0: \mu\geq\mu_0\) \(Z = Z_\alpha\)

Note that \(Z_{\alpha/2}\) and \(Z_\alpha\) refer to the upper tail Gaussian pdf areas, i.e. \[Pr(Z>Z_{\alpha/2}) = \alpha/2\mbox{ and }Pr(Z>Z_\alpha)=\alpha.\]

Hypothesis Testing for the Population Proportions

Example: Exercise Participation and Age
On average, 30% of all adults in Australia exercise weekly. A survey of \(n=100\) adults over 40 years of age revealed that 15 of the respondents indicated that they exercised weekly. Does this indicate that the proportion of adults who exercise weekly decreases with age? Assume a Type I error rate of \(0.05\).

Example: Driverless Cars
A research study showed that out of 500 individuals surveys, 276 reported that they would be willing to ride in a driverless vehicle. Test the hypothesis that over \(50\%\) of the population would be willing to ride in a driverless vehicle. Assume a Type I error rate of \(\alpha = 0.1\).

The sample statistic for the population proportion is \(\hat{p}\) which follows a sampling distribution of \[\hat{p}\sim\left(p,\frac{p(1-p)}{n}\right)\] when \(np>5\) and \(n(1-p)>5\). The resulting test is of the form \[H_0:p = p_0\qquad H_A:p\neq p_0\] or \[H_0:p \geq p_0\qquad H_A:p< p_0\] or \[H_0:p \leq p_0\qquad H_A:p> p_0\] with the rejection region defined by the test statistic for \(\hat{p}\) \[Z = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}-p_0)}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)}}\] Note that the rejection rules for the hypotheses are similar to those for the population mean.

Test for Hypotheses About the Difference in Population Means

Example: Braking Distance
Two different braking systems for cars are being evaluated. The distance in metres required to come to a complete stop from a speed of 80 km/h was measured for the two systems in an experiment consisting of 64 trials for each system. The following information was recorded

System 1 System 2
\(\bar{x} = 36.75\) \(\bar{x} = 33.53\)
\(s_1^2 = 9.48\) \(s_2^2 = 8.09\)

Is there sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis that these two systems are equally effective? Test assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\) Type I error rate.

Example: Electric Car Ranges
While there is a significant difference between the purchase price of a Tesla Y and a Nissan Leaf, is there a significant difference in the operating ranges? Real-world testing yielded these results:

Car Mean (km) Std. Dev. (km) Sample Size
Tesla Y 370 63 40
Nissan Leaf 315 47 40

Is there sufficient evidence to support the claim that the Tesla has a greater average real-world range than the Nissan Leaf? Test assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\) Type I error rate.


HINT:
Is there enough evidence to reject the hypothesis that the average real-world range of the Nissan Leaf is equal to or greater than that of the Tesla Y?

Often times the more interesting question is is there a difference between two groups, for this we need a means of testing hypotheses about the difference of two population means. In this case we would formulate the hypotheses and rejection regions as

\[ \begin{array}{cc} \mbox{Null Hypothesis} & \mbox{Rejection Region}\\ \hline H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 & |Z|>Z_{\alpha/2}\\ H_0: \mu_1\leq\mu_2 & Z>Z_\alpha\\ H_0: \mu_1\geq\mu_2 & Z = Z_\alpha \end{array} \] Note that these hypotheses are equivalent to

\[ \begin{array}{cc} \mbox{Null Hypothesis} & \mbox{Rejection Region}\\ \hline H_0: \mu_1 -\mu_2 = 0 & |Z|>Z_{\alpha/2}\\ H_0: \mu_1-\mu_2\leq 0 & Z>Z_\alpha\\ H_0: \mu_1-\mu_2\geq 0 & Z = Z_\alpha\\ \end{array} \]

and that because the labels for \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) are arbitrary the last two hypotheses with the inequalities are equivalent.

Test Statistic

The test statistic \(Z\) is previously defined for a single sample mean or proportion as \[Z_{\theta}=\frac{\hat{\theta}-\theta_0}{SE_{\hat{\theta}}}\] where \(\hat{\theta}\) is the sample estimate of the parameter \(\theta\), \(\theta_0\) is the hypothesised value of \(\theta\), and \(SE_{\hat{\theta}}\) is the standard error of \(\hat{\theta}\). In the case of hypotheses about the difference between two population means we need an estimate and its standard error. The estimator of \(\mu_1-\mu_2\) is easy to intuit as \[\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2.\] The standard error is slightly less intuitive \[SE_{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}=\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}.\] The resulting test statistic is \[Z = \frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-\Delta_0}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}\] where \(\Delta_0=\mu_1-\mu_2\).

Example: Braking Distance

Two different braking systems for cars are being evaluated. The distance in metres required to come to a complete stop from a speed of 80 km/h was measured for the two systems in an experiment consisting of 64 trials for each system. The following information was recorded

System 1 System 2
\(\bar{x} = 36.75\) \(\bar{x} = 33.53\)
\(s_1^2 = 9.48\) \(s_2^2 = 8.09\)

Is there sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis that these two systems are equally effective? Test assuming an \(\alpha = 0.05\) Type I error rate.

Solution

Because we are asked if the two systems are “equally effective” we can assume that the hypotheses are \[ H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0\qquad H_A:\:\mu_1-\mu_2\neq=0 \] where \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) are the expected stopping distance for each system. This is a two-sided test to so the rejection region is defined as \[ |Z|>Z_{\alpha/2}\equiv |Z|>1.96 \]

We are given: \[ \begin{align} \bar{x}_1&=36.75\\ \bar{x}_2&=33.53\\ s^2_1&=9.48\\ s^2_2&=8.09\\ n_1&=64\\ n_2&=64. \end{align} \] The resulting test statisitic is \[ \begin{align} Z&=\frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2-\Delta_0}{\sqrt{\frac{s^2_1}{n_1}+\frac{s^2_2}{n_2}}}\\ &=\frac{36.75-33.53-0}{\sqrt{\frac{9.48}{64}+\frac{8.09}{64}}}\\ &=\frac{3.22 }{ 0.5239573 }\\ &= 6.15 \end{align} \] because \[ 6.15>1.96 \] we reject the null hypothesis that the two systems are equivalent.

Hypotheses About the Difference Between Population Proportions

Example: Tai Chi and Fibromyalgia
There is some evidence to suggest that tai chi can alleviate chronic pain from fibromyalgia. Sixty-six individuals participated in a 12-week trial where they were divided into two equally sized groups. Group 1 participated in a tai chi class for the study period, while Group 2 participated in a wellness education class for the study period. At the end of the study, each participant was asked if they felt that their pain had reduced; a summary of the results is:

Tai Chi Wellness Education
Reduced Pain \(x_1 = 26\) \(x_2=13\)

Perform a test of the hypothesis that tai chi is more effective than the wellness class in alleviating chronic pain from fibromyalgia. Assume a Type I error rate of \(\alpha=0.1\).

Example: The Effects of Hormone Replacement Therapy and Dementia A four-year study of 4532 women conducted at 39 different medical centres divided the women at random into two equally sized groups. One group received hormone replacement therapy (HRT), and the other received a placebo. At the end of the study, there were 40 cases of dementia in the HRT group and 21 in the placebo group. Is there sufficient evidence to reject the hypothesis that the risk for dementia is lower in the HRT group? Assume a Type I error rate of \(\alpha = 0.01\).

As is the case with comparing two population means, questions about the difference between two population proportions often arise. In these cases we define the hypotheses and rejection regions as:

\[ \begin{array}{cc} \mbox{Null Hypothesis} & \mbox{Rejection Region}\\ \hline H_0: p_1 = p_2 & |Z|>Z_{\alpha/2}\\ H_0: p_1\leq p_2 & Z>Z_\alpha\\ H_0: p_1\geq p_2 & Z = Z_\alpha \end{array} \]

Note that these hypotheses are equivalent to \[ \begin{array}{cc} \mbox{Null Hypothesis} & \mbox{Rejection Region}\\ \hline H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0 & |Z|>Z_{\alpha/2}\\ H_0: p_1-p_2\leq 0 & Z>Z_\alpha\\ H_0: p_1-p_2\geq 0 & Z = Z_\alpha \end{array} \]

Test Statistic

The test statistic of comparing two population proportions differs slightly from that used to compare two populations means. The null hypotheses assume that \(p_1=p_2\) (regardless of the inequality), thus while the point estimator for the difference in proportions in \[\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2\] the standard error is \[SE_{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2}=\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}\] where \[p_0 = \frac{x_1+x_2}{n_1+n_2}\] which follows as the best estimate of \(p=p_1=p_2\). The resulting test statistic is then \[Z=\frac{\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}.\] Note that in the event that the hypothesised difference is not \(0\), i.e. \(p_1-p_2=\Delta_0\) where \(\Delta\neq 0\), the test statistic is \[Z=\frac{(\hat{p}_1-\hat{p}_2)-\Delta_0}{\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1}+\frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}}.\] This statistic is rarely used, but the evaluation and interpretation of the test results are the same.

Example: Tai Chi and Fibromyalgia

There is some evidence to suggest that tai chi can alleviate chronic pain from fibromyalgia. Sixty-six individuals participated in a 12-week trial where they were divided into two equally sized groups. Group 1 participated in a tai chi class for the study period, while Group 2 participated in a wellness education class for the study period. At the end of the study, each participant was asked if they felt that their pain had reduced; a summary of the results is:

Tai Chi Wellness Education
Reduced Pain \(x_1 = 26\) \(x_2=13\)

Perform a test to determine if tai chi is (statistically) more effective than the wellness class in alleviating chronic pain from fibromyalgia. Assume a Type I error rate of \(\alpha=0.1\).

Solution

The hypotheses are \[ H_0:\:p_1-p_2\leq 0\qquad H_A:\:p_1-p_2>0 \] That is that the proportion of patients who did Tai Chi and experienced reduced pain (\(p_1\)) is less than or equal to the proportion of patients who took the wellness education classes (\(p_2\)).

Additional Considerations

Beyond the mechanics of performing hypothesis testing, there are additional considerations that should be borne in mind. Hypothesis testing is one of the first of techniques that make up the statistical “toolbox”, and coincidentally is one of the first where we see that there is some measure of subjective interpretation needed to use and understand the results of a hypothesis test fully.

Power and Sample Size Selection

Example (cont’d): Ore Refining Operations
An ore processing facility has a record of producing an average of \(880\) tons of refined ore per day. Process engineers would like to see if this has changed over time. The records from \(n=50\) days randomly selected over the last year indicate that the sample average is \(\bar{x}=871\) tons with a standard deviation of \(21\) tons. Assuming a Type I error rate of \(\alpha = 0.05\), find the Type II error rate and power for the statistical test for a true value of \(\theta^*=875\).

Recall that we noted that the Type II error rate \(\beta\) is the probability that we fail to reject the null hypothesis given that it is false. Power is \(1-\beta\), or the probability that we reject the null hypothesis given that it is false. Computing the Type II error rate depends on the difference between the null hypothesis and the true state of reality, and the Type I error rate.

In terms of the test and rejection region, assume that we want to calculate the Type II error rate for a true value of \(\theta=\theta^*\), and a Type I error rate of \(\alpha\). If \[\beta = Pr(|Z|\leq Z_{\alpha/2}|\theta=\theta^*)\] where the test statistic \(Z\) is computed under the assumptions of the null hypothesis \[Z = \frac{\hat{\theta}-\theta_0}{SE_{\hat{\theta}}}.\] Thus power can be defined as \[\begin{aligned} \mbox{Power}& = &1-Pr(|Z|\leq Z_{\alpha/2}|\theta=\theta^*)\\ &=&Pr(|Z|\geq Z_{\alpha/2}|\theta=\theta^*)\end{aligned}\]

Assuming a Type I error rate of \(\alpha = 0.05\), find the Type II error rate and power for the statistical test for a true value of \(\theta^*=875\).

Solution

Note that the power is defined as: \[ \begin{aligned} \mbox{Power}& = 1-Pr(|Z|\leq Z_{\alpha/2}|\theta=\theta^*)\\ &=Pr(|Z|\geq Z_{\alpha/2}|\theta=\theta^*). \end{aligned} \] We are given: \[ \begin{align} \bar{x} & = 871\\ s & = 21\\ n & = 50\\ \alpha & = 0.05\\ \theta^* & = 875 \end{align} \] The Type II error rate is \[ \beta = Pr(|Z|<Z_{\alpha/2}|\theta = \theta^*). \] The first thing to note is that \(\bar{x} = 871\) is superfluous to the power calculation. The Type II error rate is simply the probability that \(\bar{x}\) is not in the rejection region give that the true value of \(\theta=\theta^*\),

The rejection region is \[ \mbox{Rejection Region}=\left\{\bar{x}:\bar{x}>\theta_0+Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\:\cap\:\bar{x}<\theta_0-Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right\} \] the Type II error rate is then \[ \beta=Pr\left(\theta_0-Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}<\bar{x}<\theta_0+Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \right). \] Note that \[ \bar{x}\sim N\left(\theta^*,\frac{s^2}{n}\right) \] hence \[ Z=\frac{\bar{x}-\theta^*}{s/\sqrt{n}} \] so the expression for the Type II error rate becomes

\[ \begin{align} \beta&=Pr\left(\theta_0-Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}<\bar{x}<\theta_0+Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \right)\\ &=Pr\left(\theta_0-Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}<\bar{x}<\theta_0+Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\Biggr|\theta=\theta^* \right)\\ &=Pr\left(\frac{\theta_0-Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}-\theta^*}{s/\sqrt{n}}< \frac{\bar{x}-\theta^*}{s/\sqrt{n}}< \frac{\theta_0+Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}-\theta^*}{s/\sqrt{n}} \right)\\ &=Pr\left(\frac{\theta_0-Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}-\theta^*}{s/\sqrt{n}}< Z< \frac{\theta_0+Z_{\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}-\theta^*}{s/\sqrt{n}} \right)\\ &=Pr\left(\frac{880-1.96\frac{21}{\sqrt{50}}-875}{21/\sqrt{50}}<Z< \frac{880+1.96\frac{21}{\sqrt{50}}-875}{21/\sqrt{50}} \right)\\ &=Pr\left(-0.28 <Z< 3.64 \right)\\ &=0.61 \end{align} \] and the power is \[ \begin{align} \mbox{Power}&=1-\beta\\ &=1-0.61\\ &=0.39 \end{align} \]

Computing Sample Size from Power

Example (cont’d): Ore Refining Operations
An ore processing facility has a record of producing an average of \(880\) tons of refined ore per day. Process engineers would like to see if this has changed over time. The records from \(n=50\) days randomly selected over the last year indicate that the sample average is \(\bar{x}=871\) tons with a standard deviation of \(21\) tons. For a Type II error rate of \(\beta =0.2\) and a Type I error rate of \(\alpha=0.05\) find the minimum sample size \(n\) for a true value of \(\theta^*=875\).

As we can see from the equations for computing power, as \(n\) increases, for a given difference between the truth and the null hypothesis, the Type II error rate decreases, hence the power increases. So we can see that sample size is a crucial design consideration when collecting data, or designing an experiment.

By choosing a minimum detectable difference between the null hypothesis and the truth and Type I and Type II error rates, the equations for computing the Type II error rate can be modified to solve for \(n\).

Solution

From the power computations we can see that \[ \begin{align} \beta&=Pr\left(\frac{880-1.96\frac{21}{\sqrt{n}}-875}{21/\sqrt{n}}<Z< \frac{880+1.96\frac{21}{\sqrt{n}}-875}{21/\sqrt{n}} \right)\\ &=Pr\left(\frac{5-1.96\frac{21}{\sqrt(n)}}{21/\sqrt{n}}<Z<\frac{5+1.96\frac{21}{\sqrt(n)}}{21/\sqrt{n}} \right)\\ &=Pr\left(5\sqrt{n}/21-1.96<Z<5\sqrt{n}/21+1.96 \right) \end{align} \] we can write our objective in terms of solving the equation \[ 0.2\geq Pr\left(5\sqrt{n}/21-1.96<Z<5\sqrt{n}/21+1.96 \right) \] for \(n\). Hypothesis Testing as the Inverse of a Confidence Interval

Example (cont’d): Protein Intake
A sample of \(n=50\) adult men reveals that their average daily intake of protein is \(\bar{x}=75.6\) grams per day with a standard deviation of \(s=3.5\) grams. Construct a (\(1-\alpha = 95\%\)) confidence interval for the average daily intake of protein for men and use it to test the hypotheses: \[H_0: \mu = 74\qquad H_A:\mu\neq 74\] (note that the (\(1-\alpha\)) confidence level corresponds to a Type I error rate of \(\alpha\).

There is a fundamental connection between confidence intervals and hypothesis tests. Both are based on probabilistic statements based on the sampling distribution for a statistic. The hypothesis test can be viewed as the complement of a confidence interval, as the rejection region defined by the hypothesis test is the compliment of the confidence interval for a two sided-test. The decision to reject the null hypothesis because it falls within the rejection region is equivalent to computing the test statistic and rejecting the null hypothesis if the value \(\theta_0\) falls outside the confidence interval.

Example (cont’d): Protein Intake

A sample of \(n=50\) adult men reveals that their average daily intake of protein is \(\bar{x}=75.6\) grams per day with a standard deviation of \(s=3.5\) grams. \(p\)-values

Example (cont’d): Driverless Cars
A research study showed that out of 500 individuals surveys, 276 reported that they would be willing to ride in a driverless vehicle. How strong is the evidence that over \(50\%\) of the population would be willing to ride in a driverless vehicle. Compute the resulting \(p\)-value

Performing a hypothesis test based on computing a rejection region and making a straightforward reject or fail to reject decision has some drawbacks. For one the choice of Type I error rate is somewhat arbitrary, and for marginal values (i.e. test statistic values close to, but not in the rejection region) decisions may not be as confident as desired. To address these issues a more subjective alternative to testing is sometimes desirable, that is to instead of constructing rejection regions a priori, test statistics are computed and evaluated as to the strength of the evidence in favour of rejecting the null hypothesis. This measure is referred to as a \(p\)-value. Quite simply put, the \(p\)-value is equal to the Type I error rate calculated for rejection region described by the observed test statistic.

Caveats and Food for Thought

Accept or Fail to Reject?

There is some debate about whether or not to fail to reject the null hypothesis constitutes tacit acceptance of the null hypothesis or if it implies that evidence concerning the null hypothesis is inconclusive. Some textbooks state that failure to reject is acceptance of the null hypothesis. The argument I present here is that if the parameter \(\theta\) can take on infinite possible values, then accepting a hypothesis that \(\theta\) equals a hypothesised value is equivalent to assuming that there is a non-zero probability that a continuous random variable will take on a given value. This is hardly a rigorous argument, but it represents a conservative approach to interpreting hypothesis tests.

Statistical Significance and Practical Importance

Statistical significance is not always an indicator of practical importance. Powerful tests can reject null hypotheses when the parameter estimator is not that different from the hypothesised value. It is important to consider what is meaningful in terms of the actual application being analysed to determine what a significant difference is. This can be an important consideration when designing tests and choosing sample sizes.

Small Sample Sampling Statistic Distribution: Student’s \(t\)-distribution

We’ve looked at methods for estimating intervals and testing hypotheses based on the sampling distribution for estimates based on large samples. The definition of large, is somewhat subjective, but the general assumption is that \(n\geq 30\) is large enough in most cases to assume that the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x}\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)\), even when we had to substitute the estimate \(s^2\) for \(\sigma^2\).

William Sealy Gossett

alt text


William Sealy Gosset was and English statistician and scientist who worked for the Guiness Brewing Company in the early twentieth century. Gosset discovered that when working with small samples estimating the variance resulted in higher Type I error rates than thought. He developed the \(t\) distribution to account for this but his employer considered it a trade secret and Gosset was forced to publish his findings under the name “Student” in order to avoid detection. Today this distribution is known as Student’s \(t\) distribution.

But this assumption of Gaussian behaviour poses two problems, first often times collecting \(30\) or more samples in impractical, and second the assumption of the Gaussian sampling distribution doesn’t take into account the extra variability due to the estimation of \(\sigma^2\). In short, when \(n\) is small, then the Gaussian assumption can fail as \[ \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{x}-\mu)}{s}\neq \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{x}-\mu)}{\sigma}. \] Instead we need a new probability distribution that takes into account the extra variability caused by using the estimate \(s\) in lieu of \(\sigma\). When \(n\) is small (\(<30\)), then it can be shown that \[ \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{x}-\mu)}{s}\stackrel{d}{\rightarrow} t_{\nu} \] where the degrees of freedom \(\nu\) is equal to \(n-1\). Student’s \(t\) distribution in a symmetric distribution that is superficially similar to the Gaussian distribution, but has heavier tails, but as \(\nu\) increases Student’s \(t\) distribution looks more and more Gaussian. Note that for \(X\sim t_{\nu}\) \[ E(X) = 0,\mbox{ and } Var(X)=\frac{\nu}{\nu-2},\:\nu>2 \] where the variance is greater than \(1\), thus the heavier tails compared with the standard Gaussian distribution \(N(0,1)\), but that as \(\nu\) (or \(n\)) increases then the variance approaches \(1\) implying that the two densities converge as \(n\) approaches infinity.

Student’s \(t\) distribution is more reasonable (and conservative) to use when computing confidence intervals or performing hypothesis testing on small samples. To do this we note that the sampling density of the quantity \[t=\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{x}-\mu)}{s}\sim t_{\nu}\] Thus for the hypotheses \[H_0: \mu=\mu_0\qquad\mbox{and}\qquad H_A:\mu\neq \mu_0\] we would reject the null hypothesis if the test statistic \[t=\left|\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{x}-\mu_0)}{s}\right| >t_{\nu,\alpha/2}\] where \(t_{\nu,\alpha/2}\) is a critical value computed using statistical software or tables such that \[Pr(t>t_{\nu,\alpha/2}>\alpha/2).\] Similarly, a small-sample confidence interval can be computed as \[ \bar{x}\pm t_{\nu,\alpha/2}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}. \]

To use this table find critical values by finding the row corresponding to \(\nu\), the degrees of freedom required, and then read across to the column corresponding to the critical value based on the confidence level or the Type I Error Rate. Note that in the case of single-sided hypotheses, the critical value for the test statistic is \(t_{\nu,\alpha}\).

Solution

The hypotheses are \[ H_0: \mu_1=\mu_2\quad vs\quad H_A: \mu_1\neq \mu_2 \] so the test statistics will be \[ T = \frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}. \] given \[ \begin{align} x_1&=1.25, 1.16, 1.33, 1.15, 1.23, 1.2, 1.32, 1.28, 1.21\\ x_2&=0.89, 1.01, 0.97, 0.95, 0.94, 1.02, 0.98, 1.06, 0.98\\ s_1^2&=0\\ s_2^2&=0\\ \nu&=14.997\approx 15 \end{align} \] \[ \begin{align} T &= \frac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1}+\frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}\\ &= \frac{1.24 - 0.98} {\sqrt{\frac{0.0037}{9}+\frac{0.0022}{9}}}\\ &=\frac{0.26}{\sqrt{4.0987654\times 10^{-4}+2.4142661\times 10^{-4}}}\\ & =\frac{0.26}{\sqrt{6.5130316\times 10^{-4}}}\\ &=\frac{0.26}{0.0255206}\\ & = 10.14 \end{align} \] and the critical value for \(t\) is \(2.13\). Thus we would reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a difference between the two materials.

Inference About the Difference in Population Means for Small Samples: Paired Differences

The procedures outlined are appropriate when samples are independent, but in many cases we want to compare two dependent samples. To illustrate the difference in these approaches consider the following example.

Example: Tyre Wear
To test the difference between two types of tyre (Type A and Type B), one tyre of each type is selected at random and then fitted to the rear wheels of one of five cars. Each car is driven for a specified number of kilometres and the observed wear is measured.

\(\bar{x}_1 = 10.24\) \(\bar{x}_2 = 9.76\)
\(s_1 = 1.316\) \(s_2 = 1.328\)

If we treat these samples as independent and perform a two-sample \(t\) test of the hypotheses \[H_0: \mu_1-\mu_2 = 0\qquad\mbox{and}\qquad H_A:\mu_1-\mu_2\neq 0\] the resulting test statistic is \[t = \frac{(\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2)-0}{\sqrt{s^2_p\left(\frac{1}{n_1}+\frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}\] where \[s_p^2=\frac{(4)(1.316)^2+(4)(1.328)^2}{8}=1.748\] \[t = \frac{(10.24-9.76)}{\sqrt{1.748\left(\frac{1}{5}+\frac{1}{5}\right)}}=0.57\] Assuming a Type I Error Rate of \(\alpha = 0.05\), the critical value for testing our hypothesis is \(t_{8, 0.975}\) which we can find from the table (or from qt(0.975,8)) as \(2.306\). As \(|0.57|<2.306\) we would fail to reject the hypothesis and conclude that there is insufficient evidence of a difference between the two types of tyre.

However we notice that every measurement for Tyre A is greater than the corresponding measurement for Tyre B. And recall that each measurement is for the same car, so for each car, Tyre A shows greater wear than for Type B. This cast some suspicion on the results of our independent two-sample \(t\) test, and seems to indicate that there is a difference between the two types of tyre.

It turns out that there is an “error” in the experimental design. If the experimenter had treated each wheel as a unit for random allocation, that is allocated tyres to each of the ten wheels (five cars, two wheels each) randomly (so that for instance a car might have two of the same type of tyres), then the two samples would be independent. As it stands, because the experimenter allocated one type of tyre to each car the results are paired or matched. In this case the data that should be considered is the difference in wear patterns between the tyres on each car. This is referred to a paired-difference or matched difference design. In this case the data of interest are:

and \(s^2_d = 0.007\) If \(\mu_d\) is the population difference in tyre wear, the hypotheses for testing are: \[H_0:\mu_d = 0\qquad\mbox{and}\qquad H_A:\mu_d\neq 0\] with the resulting test statistic \[t = \frac{\bar{d}-0}{s_d/\sqrt{n}}=\frac{0.48}{0.837/\sqrt{5}}=12.8\] assuming a Type I Error Rate of \(\alpha = 0.05\) the critical value for \(\nu=4\) is \(2.776\), therefore because \(12.8>2.776\) we would reject the null hypothesis and conclude that there is a difference in the wear for the two types of tyres.

Note that a similar procedure can be used for computing a confidence interval \[\bar{d}\pm t_{\nu,\alpha/2}\frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}.\]

This is an important example of how simple changes to an experiment and violating assumptions can lead to very misleading results.
